Heuristic Evaluation

The following heuristic evaluation of the CUA Tower website is based on the task of looking for material based on the topics of drug and tobacco use, and smoking on the Catholic University campus in the role of a student looking for information for an undergraduate course assignment. The heuristics used are from Jakob Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics.

Heuristic System Evaluation
Visibility of System Status
  • On click or hover, the site responds within a second or two by directing the user to a new page.
  • After a user is redirected to a new page, the images on the page are slow to render and it is unclear if this is due to an error on the page or not.
  • Because of the lack of clear placeholder images, it is unclear if some articles don’t include header photos or if the site just will not load them.
Match Between the System and the Real World
  • The language and conventions used on the site are simple and fit the expectations of what would be found on any news site.
  • Tags and topic labels are straightforward (e.g. News, Featured, A&E, Sports, Politics, Editor’s Picks, What’s New) so that, despite there being very little explanatory language, site navigation is intuitive.
  • The archives are arranged according to year and month, which is intuitive and allows for quick access.
User Control and Freedom
  • Use of the breadcrumb menus on each page of the site makes it simple to get back to previous or parent pages, and allows the user to quickly form an understanding of the layout of the website and its hierarchy.
  • There is no opportunity to “get lost” since each page essentially allows the user to navigate back to the home page or explore the parent page, or topic, of whatever page they are on.
Consistency and Standards
  • The general layout of the site and its major topics (News, A&E, Sports, Politics), as well as the layout of the articles (comments, a header photo, a publication date, and an author), follow the general conventions of what would be expected from any online news outlet.
  • Within the site there are inconsistencies between the topic pages. For example, the articles on the “Politics” page are laid out like the home, search result, and archival pages, that is, in a grid structure with a header photo for each article, while the pages for “News”, “Arts & Entertainment”, and “Sports” are text-based. Therefore the articles listed under topics other than “Politics” are not immediately recognizable as articles.
Error Prevention
  • There were no ways for users to encounter errors on the site. Other than a consistent issue with images rendering slowly, the site is naturally not error-prone due to user activities being limited to searching and browsing.
Recognition Rather than Recall
  • The persistent navigation bar and breadcrumb menus on each page make navigating the site simple and intuitive. While searching there is very little to keep track of other than using the browser to bookmark articles. The navigation bar is straightforward with minimal nesting and the labels are common terms.
  • There are only a few options for actions that a user can execute: viewing articles, leaving comments, and performing simple searches.
Flexibility and Efficiency of Use
  • After viewing a few articles, it becomes intuitive to use the breadcrumb menu on each article and page to navigate back and forth between related pages rather than using the navigation menu. This offers a browsing shortcut to more experienced users.
Aesthetic and Minimalist Design
  • The header is key-catching, but simple enough in its limited use of colors to not be overwhelming.
  • The home page contains multiple components, images, and animations, which can be overwhelming for users looking for articles on a specific topic. The scrolling “Flash Story” feature is eye-catching, but adds to the general “busyness” of the page.
  • It is not clear how the different units of articles are differentiated from each other on the home page. Stories appearing in different sections appear to be the same stories. For example, it is unclear what the difference is between “Main Stories” and “Featured”.
  • The archive side panel is long and much of the vertical space of the pages on the site is devoted to unnecessary white space at the bottom of the page.
Help Users Recognize, Diagnose, and Recover from Errors
  • Searching for a non-existent search term in the search bar results in a “Nothing Found” page. This page informs the user that nothing matched their search term, a user-friendly message that explains why no search results were returned. The page also includes messaging that suggests the user “Please try again with some different keywords.”
Help and Documentation
  • There is no help text – embedded or otherwise – available on the site. Which made determining how to use the search bar very difficult and resulted in ambiguous search results.
  • The different topics, under which articles are grouped, do not include descriptions for the scope of each topic. For example, it is not clear if “News” means campus news or includes news from the greater DC area.
  • It is also not clear why articles are presented in the order that they are under each topic, since the article listings do not include dates. Are these articles from the current month? The most recent articles? Or featured articles?
  • Articles on topic pages also do not include helpful tags and the snippets under each article are excerpts of the page text, which often includes unhelpful information such as the header photo description and the photo courtesy.

Strengths and Weaknesses

Strengths

  • Simplicity and Limited Actions - The general design of the website is intuitive and, not counting the home page, simple to understand without any confusing or distracting embellishments. The site presents itself in the fashion of other major news sites with familiar topics (e.g. News, Arts and Entertainment, Politics, Sports, Cartoons & Crosswords) and does not include any additional pages that do not relate directly to articles or other recognizable, administrative functions of an online newspaper, such as donations and subscriptions. Articles are presented with header photos, which provide context to the story within, and in a grid structure that facilitates skimming through articles on the search results page. There are no opportunities for users to run into errors as there are only four main activities that users can perform on the site: browsing, viewing articles, leaving comments, and performing simple searches.
  • Ease of Navigation - Navigation between pages is simplified through the use of the breadcrumb menu included on each page. This feature allows users to understand where they are within the site and easily navigate up and down its tree structure to either get back to the page they were at previously, or explore and browse related pages. More experienced users may find this feature more intuitive than using the navigation bar, allowing the breadcrumb menu to act as a shortcut

Weaknesses

  • Context and Adaption - One major weakness of the site is its lack of context, and this is the result of there being no inclusion of help text explaining the context for which the articles are displayed. Each topic has a page wherein articles tagged for that topic are listed, but these pages do not include descriptions for the scope of each topic. If a user navigates to the “News” page, it is not immediately clear if the articles pertain to campus news or include news from the DC area, or nationwide. There is a simple search bar on each page, including the topic pages and the home page, but it is not clear whether these searches will be executed on the whole site or just the context of the topic, if the search bar is displayed on a topic page. It is also unclear in what order articles are displayed under each topic. Are these articles from the current month? The most recent? Are these featured articles? If so, why are they featured? What is the difference between stories that are featured and an article that is an “Editor’s Pick”? The absence of any explanation for why these articles are being displayed leads to confusion and frustration during browsing, and risks making the topic headers in the navigation bar functionally useless.
  • Lack of Advanced Search Capabilities - The CUA Tower website lacks advanced search capabilities. The simple search bar is the only way to search the site and does not include any advanced search features or help text indicating how keywords or phrases will be interpreted by the site. Therefore it is not clear how searches are being executed and leaves the user without an understanding of how to build their search strings. The search results page does not offer faceted filters or a way to filter based on tags, even though tags are visible for each article in the search results. When accessing stories from the search results page, the next and previous stories listed on the articles are not in the context of the search results. The search results do not seem to be in any particular order and there is nothing on the search results page to indicate why certain articles have been included. To ensure that relevant articles are not missed, a student or instructor needs to explore every single page of the results, which is not ideal for time-sensitive tasks.

Part 2: Usability Test

Results of Usability Test

Many of the points noted in the usability test aligned with what was observed from my heuristic evaluation. The volunteer noted how overstimulating the home page of the website was, but appreciated the organization of the archives. They did not agree with my assessment on the simplicity of the rest of the site, finding it both lacking in content (e.g. too simple) and noting that they did not like the organization of the articles as it was hard to focus on one thing on each topic page. Though most of the frustration indicated by the volunteer during the usability test was due to the lack of control and context in the search functionality. Given the task of looking for articles, it was natural for the volunteer to automatically resort to using the search bar and look for articles with particular keywords. The volunteer had never used the site before and finding information via browsing would have been more time-consuming. After a few executed searches, the lack of clarity in the search results became a major point of frustration for the volunteer and they focused on this point while commenting on the usability of the site. Ultimately, the poor search results prevented them from completing the research task. They did not understand why certain results were returned or how they related to the keywords. They indicated that their search would have ended early given that they did not know how to continue. This reaction aligns with my analysis that the lack of advanced search features are not ideal for research, and the need to search each article for relevant information is too time consuming of a process to make the search offered on the site of any use to students.

Suggestions for Improvement

Because of the ubiquity of search engines and the advanced features offered on library and research databases, it is vital that the CUA Tower team invest in enhancing the search features included on their site in order to make their articles usable beyond browsing. These limitations can be corrected with a few suggestions. First, add help text to the search bar explaining how searches are executed and use tooltips or modals to offer detailed guides for how users should structure their queries. Second, include an option for advanced searching using Boolean operators that allow users to search within specific fields such as author, body, title, and tags. Third, add faceted filters on the search results page so users can further refine their searches. These features would be more functional by the implementation of granular tagging. Currently tags are limited to broad topics (e.g. News, Politics, A&E, Featured) and should be expanded to include terms from a controlled vocabulary, defined by the editors, that can be used in filtering and searching.

I would also suggest that the design of the home page be revisited and simplified so that animations are limited to only one element, rather than the three elements on the current version of the home page. Tooltips should also be implemented to explain the purpose and scope behind each grouping of articles, these UI elements are unobtrusive and therefore can be ignored by experienced site users and used throughout the topic pages to add context or help text where needed. The format of the “Politics” page should also be replicated for the other topic pages so that the website maintains its uniformity in how it presents articles. It is also worth considering adding faceted search and filtering to each topic page to enable better browsing, but, as with the suggestions for enhanced search features, this functionality would only be effective with the implementation of a controlled vocabulary and granular tagging.
